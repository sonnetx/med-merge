\begin{abstract}
Weight-space model merging promises a single deployable model from multiple fine-tuned specialists, but it is unclear how reliably it works for medical imaging, where tasks span nearly disjoint visual domains (radiology, dermoscopy, histopathology, colon pathology, fundus). We run a controlled study across 7 ViT backbones, 6 medical imaging datasets, 8 merging methods, and 3 seeds, evaluated against an oracle-router upper bound and a multi-task joint-training (MTL) baseline. Three findings. (i) Merge forgetting is overwhelmingly a property of the target dataset, not of the method or the backbone, with per-dataset mean forgetting spanning $0.06$ (radiology) to $0.71$ (PathMNIST) and dataset+method indicators alone explaining LOBO median $R^2 = 0.79$ across seeds. (ii) PathMNIST exhibits a uniform 7-backbone collapse, where near-saturated specialists ($\geq 0.99$ balanced accuracy) drop to $0.63$ under the best merge, a 37-point loss with no backbone escaping it. RetinaMNIST and TCGA also show substantial gaps (27 and 9 points respectively), while radiology, dermoscopy, and CheXpert merge near-losslessly. (iii) Where an MTL baseline exists (the original 4 datasets), MTL ties or beats the best merge throughout, suggesting joint training is the safer default when feasible. Within these findings, task-vector cosine similarity is informative for compute triage but does not generalize as a cross-backbone predictor of merge success.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Motivation}

A single radiology department may need separate classifiers for chest X-ray multilabel screening, dermoscopy lesion classification, and histopathology subtyping. Deploying and maintaining a separate ViT per task is expensive. Weight-space merging, which combines multiple fine-tuned models' parameters into a single multi-task model without retraining \cite{ilharco2023editing,yadav2023ties,yu2024language,wortsman2022modelsoupsaveragingweights}, has emerged as an efficient alternative. Merging is attractive in clinical deployments because (a) it requires no joint access to the training data, addressing institutional privacy constraints, and (b) it produces a model the same size as one specialist.

Existing merging work, however, is dominated by tasks with relatively low cross-domain gap (different NLP datasets, ImageNet-style image-classification benchmarks). Medical imaging is structurally different. Dermoscopy, chest radiography, and histopathology are nearly disjoint visual domains, and within each dataset there is additional shift from institutions, scanners, and demographics (CheXpert is Stanford, NIH ChestX-ray is NIH, TCGA and ISIC span dozens of contributing sites, PathMNIST is multi-source colon-pathology scans). It is unclear whether prevailing merging methods preserve specialist quality under such heterogeneity, whether the choice of pretrained backbone matters, and whether the choice of merging method should depend on the target task.

We run a controlled study across up to 7 backbones (CLIP, ViT, DINOv3, RAD-DINO, DINOv2, ViT-MAE, BEiT), up to 6 datasets (ISIC 2017, CheXpert, NIH ChestX-ray, TCGA-LUAD/LUSC, PathMNIST, RetinaMNIST), and 8 merging methods \cite{ilharco2023editing,yadav2023ties,yu2024language,matena2022mergingmodelsfisherweightedaveraging,wortsman2022modelsoupsaveragingweights,du2024parametercompetitionbalancingmodel,wang2025linesposttraininglayerscaling}, evaluated against an oracle-router upper bound and a multi-task joint-training (MTL) baseline, with three random seeds throughout. We ask where merging preserves specialist quality and where it collapses, and what (if anything) separates the two regimes. Three plausible factors compete, namely the choice of merging method, the target dataset, and the geometry of the underlying task vectors.

Our central contribution is to characterize the cross-dataset variance in merging behavior in medical imaging, identify a catastrophic single-dataset failure mode (PathMNIST) that persists across every backbone we tested, and quantify where joint training (MTL) is a stronger default than merging. As an auxiliary analysis, we test whether task-vector cosine geometry generalizes as a cross-backbone predictor of merge forgetting under leave-one-backbone-out (LOBO) cross-validation, and find that it does not.

\section{Methods}

We fine-tune 7 pretrained ViT backbones (CLIP ViT-B/16, ViT-B/16, DINOv3 ViT-S/16, RAD-DINO, DINOv2-Base, ViT-MAE-Base, BEiT-Base) on 6 medical imaging datasets (ISIC 2017 dermoscopy, CheXpert and NIH ChestX-ray for 5-label radiology, TCGA-LUAD/LUSC for binary lung histopathology, PathMNIST for 9-class colon histopathology, and RetinaMNIST for 5-class fundus). Specialists use AdamW with cosine-warmup and early stopping, and CLIP requires $10\times$ lower LR.

We merge task vectors $\tau_i = \theta_{\text{ft},i} - \theta_{\text{pre}}$ across 8 methods (Simple Averaging, Task Arithmetic \cite{ilharco2023editing}, TIES \cite{yadav2023ties}, DARE \cite{yu2024language}, DARE-TIES, PCB-Merging \cite{du2024parametercompetitionbalancingmodel}, LiNeS \cite{wang2025linesposttraininglayerscaling}, and Fisher-Weighted Merging \cite{matena2022mergingmodelsfisherweightedaveraging}), each with a per-method validation-set grid search. We compare against two baselines, an oracle router (per-sample routing to the dataset specialist, the upper bound on any merged model) and multi-task joint training (MTL, a shared encoder with per-dataset heads, round-robin batching).

We define per-cell merge forgetting $\Delta_d = \text{oracle}_d - \text{merged}_d$ in each dataset's primary metric (balanced accuracy on ISIC, PathMNIST, and RetinaMNIST, macro-AUROC on CheXpert and NIH, and AUROC on TCGA). For the cross-architecture predictor analysis in \S\ref{sec:predictability}, we use leave-one-backbone-out (LOBO) cross-validation, which holds out an entire backbone and evaluates the predictor on it. This matches the regime a practitioner faces when planning a merge on a new architecture.

\subsection{Merge forgetting clusters strongly by dataset}

\bottomrule

Per-dataset mean forgetting (averaged across all 7 backbones, 8 methods, and 3 seeds) spans an order of magnitude across the 6 datasets. The radiology cluster (CheXpert and NIH) merges near-losslessly at $\approx 0.06$ regardless of backbone or method. TCGA, ISIC, and RetinaMNIST occupy a middle band with $0.18$--$0.25$ forgetting. PathMNIST is an extreme outlier at $0.71$. The within-dataset spread across methods and backbones is small relative to the between-dataset spread, so the dataset identity carries most of the predictable variance. Figure~\ref{fig:norm_vs_cos} visualizes this geometrically across all 42 (backbone, dataset) cells in the $(\|\tau\|, 1{-}\cos)$ plane, where datasets cluster vertically (color bands) more than horizontally. These forgetting numbers must be read against the per-dataset specialist baseline (Appendix~A), because absolute forgetting is interpretation-sensitive when the specialist itself is weak. RetinaMNIST specialists average only $0.49$ balanced accuracy on a 5-class ordinal task (random baseline $0.20$), so its $0.25$ absolute forgetting collapses the merged model close to chance, whereas the comparable absolute forgetting on ISIC ($0.23$) is from a higher specialist base of $0.71$.

\subsection{PathMNIST, a uniform 7-backbone collapse}
\label{sec:pathmnist}

PathMNIST is the only dataset where merging fails catastrophically, and it fails on every backbone in our study. Per-backbone mean forgetting (across all methods and seeds) ranges from $0.56$ (CLIP) to $0.87$ (DINOv2), with no backbone below $0.55$. Per-backbone specialists are near-saturated ($\geq 0.992$ balanced accuracy, Appendix~A), and across the full 42-cell grid the Spearman correlation between specialist accuracy and merge forgetting is essentially zero ($\rho = +0.02$), so the collapse is not just ``saturated specialists had more to lose.'' What makes PathMNIST distinct is geometric. Colon pathology is visually disjoint from every pretraining corpus we tested (natural images, radiology, ImageNet self-supervision), so the fine-tuned task vector is both large in norm and far in direction from the other datasets' task vectors (upper-left band of Figure~\ref{fig:norm_vs_cos}). The specialists reach near-perfect test accuracy because the dataset is sufficiently large and well-behaved for a ViT to memorize end-to-end, but the resulting solution is a narrow, dataset-specific configuration of weights that weight-space averaging destroys. The best merge across all backbones and methods reaches only $0.627$ (CLIP, LiNeS), a 37-point drop from the saturated $0.998$ ceiling. PathMNIST is qualitatively different from the rest of the grid, not just a quantitatively harder version of the same problem.

All values are seed-averaged. The best merge ties or beats the best specialist on three of six datasets (ISIC, CheXpert, NIH) with sub-percentage-point gaps, recovers within 9 points on TCGA, and collapses on the two MedMNIST datasets. We also ran an MTL baseline on the original 4 datasets (4 epochs, specialist optimizer). Per-backbone MTL is competitive with the best merge throughout (within $0.005$ of best merge on CheXpert and NIH, while merge beats MTL by 4 points on ISIC). MTL was not re-run on PathMNIST or RetinaMNIST after the grid expansion, so we cannot benchmark merge against MTL on the worst-merging datasets.

\subsection{No merging method dominates}
\label{sec:method-ranking}

Within each dataset, the best-performing merging method varies. Fisher-weighted merging wins ISIC, DARE-TIES wins the radiology cluster, and PCB-Merging wins lung histopathology (TCGA), consistent with the methods literature \cite{yadav2023ties,yu2024language,du2024parametercompetitionbalancingmodel}. The lift from picking the best method per cell over a fixed default ($\alpha=0.3$ Task Arithmetic) is comparable to the within-dataset variance, so per-cell hyperparameter search is necessary in practice. We treat method ranking as a routine empirical exercise rather than a contribution.

\subsection{Cosine geometry as a cross-architecture predictor}
\label{sec:predictability}

A natural question, given the strong per-dataset structure, is whether task-vector cosine similarity carries cross-architecture signal about which (backbone, dataset, method) cells will collapse. We test this under leave-one-backbone-out (LOBO) cross-validation. Dataset+method dummies alone deliver a strong predictor (median LOBO $R^2 = 0.79$, Spearman $\rho = 0.78$ across seeds), confirming that the dataset effect is largely encoded by indicator variables. Adding the cosine feature contributes essentially nothing on average (median $\Delta R^2 = -0.002$); per-seed permutation tests show small lifts that are highly significant in opposite directions across seeds (per-seed details in Appendix~B). The honest reading is that cosine geometry is informative within a backbone (pooled Spearman $\rho \approx 0.50$ on the 42-cell grid) but does not behave as a generalizable cross-backbone predictor.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{lobo_predictions.png}
\caption{LOBO predictions vs.\ observed forgetting from cosine + dataset/method dummies, pooled across all 3 seeds. Color encodes held-out backbone. Dashed line is $y=x$ and dotted line is the global mean.}
\label{fig:lobo_predictions}
\end{figure}

Cosine is still useful operationally even without forecasting power. Routing the bottom-half-cosine cells to the specialist oracle instead of running a merge sweep gives mean primary metric $0.741$ vs full-merge $0.622$ on the 42-cell grid at 50\% compute saved (Appendix~B), driven by routing the catastrophic PathMNIST and RetinaMNIST cells to their specialists.

\section{Discussion}

The dataset-effect-first reading of these results gives a practitioner playbook, with the important caveat that absolute forgetting needs to be read against the per-dataset specialist baseline (Appendix~A). On same-domain or near-pretraining-distribution tasks (radiology, CheXpert, ISIC), specialists reach $0.71$--$0.86$ and merging is essentially free, with the choice of method barely mattering. On TCGA, specialists reach $0.77$ and merging recovers most of the signal (merged $0.75$, a 22\% relative loss), and per-cell method tuning matters. On RetinaMNIST, the specialist itself is weak ($0.49$ on a 5-class ordinal task whose chance level is $0.20$), and merging pushes the model down to $0.35$, which is closer to chance than to the specialist; calling this a ``27-point gap'' is technically accurate but understates that we are dealing with a regime where neither specialists nor merges work well. On PathMNIST, specialists are near-perfect but the task vector is geometrically extreme, no merging method or backbone in our zoo recovers the specialist, and any deployment plan that involves a colon-histopathology head should either skip merging entirely (route to a specialist) or use MTL. Where we can compare merging to MTL directly (the original 4 datasets), MTL is competitive throughout, so when joint training is feasible MTL is the safer default. Cosine geometry, despite not generalizing as a cross-backbone predictor, is useful as a compute-triage signal because the cells with lowest task-vector cosine are also the cells most likely to collapse, so routing them to specialists captures most of the lost accuracy at 50\% compute saved.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

The backbone zoo of $N = 7$ is small, and the jackknife SE on $\Delta R^2$ spans $0.02$--$0.30$ across seeds, dominated by single-backbone leverage. MTL coverage is incomplete, with our MTL baseline only run on the original 4 backbones $\times$ 4 datasets, so the new backbones (DINOv2, MAE, BEiT) and new datasets (PathMNIST, RetinaMNIST) do not have an MTL comparator. MTL is also undertuned (4 epochs, specialist optimizer), and we do not claim the PathMNIST or RetinaMNIST merge collapses are structural rather than tuning-driven. Some 2024-era methods (RegMean, AdaMerging, Localize-and-Stitch) are not benchmarked, and cosine requires fine-tuned specialists up front, so the predictor is not free. Scope is restricted to ViT backbones, $224\times224$ images, classification only, and one-shot rather than continual merging. Several of our datasets are themselves multi-source (TCGA, ISIC, PathMNIST), so the cross-domain shift we study is overlaid on uncontrolled within-dataset institutional and scanner shift, which we do not disentangle. The TCGA binary $F_1$ is a thresholding artifact, AUROC and balanced accuracy are unaffected, and threshold-tuning is a cheap fix we leave to future work.

\section{Fisher-wins-ISIC validation}

Fisher matrices computed on small validation sets have large magnitude, so Fisher-weighted merging concentrates on the dataset with the smallest validation set. We verify directly. ISIC's per-dataset Fisher magnitude (sum of $|F_d|$ over parameters) is between $\mathbf{120\times}$ (RAD-DINO) and $\mathbf{1000\times}$ (DINOv3) larger than the radiology datasets'. Practitioners should normalize Fisher matrices by validation-set size before merging in heterogeneous-data regimes. (Note that the Fisher diagonal computed for TCGA is exactly zero in our cache, a binary-classification edge case, so the Fisher merge on TCGA behaves as the Fisher-weighted average of the non-TCGA task vectors only.)
