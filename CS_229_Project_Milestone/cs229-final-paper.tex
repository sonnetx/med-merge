\begin{abstract}
Weight-space model merging promises a single deployable model from multiple fine-tuned specialists, but it is unclear how reliably it works for medical imaging, where tasks span nearly disjoint visual domains (radiology, dermoscopy, histopathology, colon pathology, fundus), or what predicts merge success. We run a controlled study across 7 ViT backbones, 6 medical imaging datasets, 8 merging methods, and 3 seeds, evaluated against an oracle-router upper bound and a multi-task joint-training (MTL) baseline. Three findings. (i) Dataset identity is the dominant cross-backbone predictor of merge forgetting. Dataset+method indicators alone explain LOBO median $R^2 = 0.79$ (Spearman $\rho = 0.78$) across seeds, with per-dataset mean forgetting spanning $0.06$ (radiology) to $0.71$ (PathMNIST) at the extremes. (ii) Task-vector cosine similarity contributes essentially zero predictive lift on top of these indicators, with median $\Delta R^2 = -0.002$ across seeds and per-seed permutation tests showing small lifts that flip sign between seeds, so cosine geometry does not behave as a generalizable cross-backbone predictor. (iii) The best merge ties or beats the specialist on three of six datasets, fails catastrophically on PathMNIST (37-point loss vs.\ a near-saturated specialist), and falls roughly 27 points short on RetinaMNIST and 9 points short on TCGA. On the original four datasets where we have an MTL baseline, MTL is competitive throughout. Practically, forecasting merge forgetting from task-vector geometry is harder than dataset-level inspection of where forgetting clusters. The dataset is the signal.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Motivation}

A single radiology department may need separate classifiers for chest X-ray multilabel screening, dermoscopy lesion classification, and histopathology subtyping. Deploying and maintaining a separate ViT per task is expensive. Weight-space merging, which combines multiple fine-tuned models' parameters into a single multi-task model without retraining \cite{ilharco2023editing,yadav2023ties,yu2024language,wortsman2022modelsoupsaveragingweights}, has emerged as an efficient alternative. Merging is attractive in clinical deployments because (a) it requires no joint access to the training data, addressing institutional privacy constraints, and (b) it produces a model the same size as one specialist.

Existing merging work, however, is dominated by tasks with relatively low cross-domain gap (different NLP datasets, ImageNet-style image-classification benchmarks). Medical imaging is structurally different. Dermoscopy, chest radiography, and histopathology are nearly disjoint visual domains. It is unclear whether prevailing merging methods preserve specialist quality under such heterogeneity, whether the choice of pretrained backbone matters, and whether the choice of merging method should depend on the target task.

We run a controlled study across up to 7 backbones (CLIP, ViT, DINOv3, RAD-DINO, DINOv2, ViT-MAE, BEiT), up to 6 datasets (ISIC 2017, CheXpert, NIH ChestX-ray, TCGA-LUAD/LUSC, PathMNIST, RetinaMNIST), and 8 merging methods \cite{ilharco2023editing,yadav2023ties,yu2024language,matena2022mergingmodelsfisherweightedaveraging,wortsman2022modelsoupsaveragingweights,du2024parametercompetitionbalancingmodel,wang2025linesposttraininglayerscaling}, evaluated against an oracle-router upper bound and a multi-task joint-training (MTL) baseline, with three random seeds throughout. We ask what is predictable about merge failure. Three plausible signals compete (the choice of merging method, the target dataset, and the geometry of the underlying task vectors), and we test each under leave-one-backbone-out (LOBO) cross-validation. Prior work hints at a task-vector geometry/quality link \cite{yadav2023ties,ortiz2023task} but does not quantify cross-architecture generalization, and this paper does.

Our central contribution is to identify domain heterogeneity as the dominant axis of predictable variance in merge forgetting in medical imaging, characterize how task-vector cosine similarity provides a useful within-fleet signal about mergeability, and quantify when joint training (MTL) is a stronger default than merging.

\section{Methods}

We fine-tune 7 pretrained ViT backbones (CLIP ViT-B/16, ViT-B/16, DINOv3 ViT-S/16, RAD-DINO, DINOv2-Base, ViT-MAE-Base, BEiT-Base) on 6 medical imaging datasets (ISIC 2017 dermoscopy, CheXpert and NIH ChestX-ray for 5-label radiology, TCGA-LUAD/LUSC for binary lung histopathology, PathMNIST for 9-class colon histopathology, and RetinaMNIST for 5-class fundus). Specialists use AdamW with cosine-warmup and early stopping, and CLIP requires $10\times$ lower LR.

We merge task vectors $\tau_i = \theta_{\text{ft},i} - \theta_{\text{pre}}$ across 8 methods (Simple Averaging, Task Arithmetic \cite{ilharco2023editing}, TIES \cite{yadav2023ties}, DARE \cite{yu2024language}, DARE-TIES, PCB-Merging \cite{du2024parametercompetitionbalancingmodel}, LiNeS \cite{wang2025linesposttraininglayerscaling}, and Fisher-Weighted Merging \cite{matena2022mergingmodelsfisherweightedaveraging}), each with a per-method validation-set grid search. We compare against two baselines, an oracle router (per-sample routing to the dataset specialist, the upper bound on any merged model) and multi-task joint training (MTL, a shared encoder with per-dataset heads, round-robin batching).

We predict per-cell merge forgetting $\Delta_d = \text{oracle}_d - \text{merged}_d$ in each dataset's primary metric (balanced accuracy on ISIC, PathMNIST, and RetinaMNIST, macro-AUROC on CheXpert and NIH, and AUROC on TCGA), under leave-one-backbone-out (LOBO) cross-validation. Plain leave-one-point-out leaks information through dataset/method dummies because the cosine feature is constant within a backbone, so LOBO holds out an entire backbone and evaluates the predictor on it. This matches the regime a practitioner faces when planning a merge on a new architecture.

Dataset+method dummies alone deliver a strong cross-backbone predictor (median LOBO $R^2 = 0.79$, Spearman $\rho = 0.78$), so the dataset effect on merge forgetting is highly predictable from indicator variables alone. Task-vector cosine similarity on top contributes essentially nothing on average (median $\Delta R^2 = -0.002$). A $1000$-permutation test on the cosine column gives the per-seed two-sided $p$-values above (column ``perm.\ $p$''). The observed lift is highly significant in opposite directions on seeds 123 ($+0.007$, $p = 0.001$) and 456 ($-0.019$, $p < 0.001$), and marginal at seed 42 ($p = 0.077$). The honest reading is that cosine carries a small amount of signal but is not stable in sign across seeds, so it does not behave as a generalizable cross-backbone predictor, and the dataset+method indicators capture essentially all of the predictable variance.

\subsection{Geometry of the dataset effect}
\label{sec:isic-vs-tcga}

Per-dataset mean forgetting (averaged across backbones, methods, and seeds) clusters strongly by dataset, ranging from $\approx 0.06$ on the radiology cluster up to $\approx 0.71$ on PathMNIST (full table in Appendix~B). PathMNIST is uniquely catastrophic across all seven backbones. The full 42 (backbone, dataset) scatter (Figure~\ref{fig:norm_vs_cos}) shows the dataset-level pattern but no clean $\|\tau\|$ or cosine separation, because backbones with larger task vectors also tend to produce more-aligned ones, so neither $\|\tau\|$ nor $1{-}\cos$ in isolation yields a strong rank correlation with forgetting at the 42-cell grid level.

\section{Discussion}

Three practitioner takeaways. \textbf{(i) Same-domain tasks merge trivially.} On the radiology cluster (CheXpert + NIH), simple averaging or DARE-TIES achieves forgetting $\approx 0.05$ across all backbones, so method choice barely matters and hyperopt is wasted. \textbf{(ii) Cross-domain tasks are dataset-dominated.} Forgetting tracks the dataset, not the method or backbone. On the original 4 datasets where MTL was run, MTL is competitive with the best merge throughout, and we did not re-run MTL on PathMNIST or RetinaMNIST, where merging itself collapses (mean forgetting $\approx 0.71$ on PathMNIST and $\approx 0.25$ on RetinaMNIST). When joint training is feasible, MTL is the safer default. \textbf{(iii) Cosine geometry is a within-fleet sanity check only.} Aggregated Spearman $\rho \approx 0.50$ across the 42 (backbone, dataset) cells, but no consistent cross-backbone $R^2$ contribution under honest LOBO (median $\Delta R^2 = -0.002$, with permutation tests indicating small lifts inconsistent in sign across seeds). We also report a screening ablation in which the bottom-half-cosine cells fall back to the specialist oracle, achieving mean primary metric $0.74$ vs full-merge $0.62$ on the 42-cell grid (Appendix~B), so cosine is useful for compute triage even though it does not forecast merge success on a new architecture.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

The backbone zoo of $N = 7$ is small, and the jackknife SE on $\Delta R^2$ spans $0.02$--$0.30$ across seeds, dominated by single-backbone leverage. MTL coverage is incomplete, with our MTL baseline only run on the original 4 backbones $\times$ 4 datasets, so the new backbones (DINOv2, MAE, BEiT) and new datasets (PathMNIST, RetinaMNIST) do not have an MTL comparator. MTL is also undertuned (4 epochs, specialist optimizer), and we do not claim the PathMNIST or RetinaMNIST merge collapses are structural rather than tuning-driven. Some 2024-era methods (RegMean, AdaMerging, Localize-and-Stitch) are not benchmarked, and cosine requires fine-tuned specialists up front, so the predictor is not free. Scope is restricted to ViT backbones, $224\times224$ images, classification only, single-institution data, and one-shot rather than continual merging. The TCGA binary $F_1$ is a thresholding artifact, AUROC and balanced accuracy are unaffected, and threshold-tuning is a cheap fix we leave to future work.

\section{Fisher-wins-ISIC validation}

Fisher matrices computed on small validation sets have large magnitude, so Fisher-weighted merging concentrates on the dataset with the smallest validation set. We verify directly. ISIC's per-dataset Fisher magnitude (sum of $|F_d|$ over parameters) is between $\mathbf{120\times}$ (RAD-DINO) and $\mathbf{1000\times}$ (DINOv3) larger than the radiology datasets'. Practitioners should normalize Fisher matrices by validation-set size before merging in heterogeneous-data regimes. (Note that the Fisher diagonal computed for TCGA is exactly zero in our cache, a binary-classification edge case, so the Fisher merge on TCGA behaves as the Fisher-weighted average of the non-TCGA task vectors only.)
