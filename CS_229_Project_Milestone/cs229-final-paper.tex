\begin{abstract}
Weight-space merging promises multiple specialist capabilities in a single model, but it is unclear how reliably it works for medical imaging, where tasks span nearly disjoint visual domains (radiology, dermoscopy, histopathology). We run a controlled study across 7 ViT backbones, 4 medical imaging datasets, 8 merging methods, and 3 seeds, evaluated against an oracle-router upper bound and a multi-task joint-training (MTL) baseline. We present three key findings. (i) Merge forgetting is overwhelmingly a property of the target dataset, not of the method or the backbone, with per-dataset mean forgetting spanning $0.06$ (radiology) to $0.23$ (dermoscopy) and dataset+method indicators dominating cross-architecture predictability. (ii) Where merging fails most (TCGA, a 9-point gap to the specialist), MTL closes roughly half the gap, suggesting joint training can escape a geometric failure mode that weight-space averaging cannot. (iii) On the radiology cluster (CheXpert and NIH), all three approaches sit within a percentage point of each other and the choice is driven by deployment constraints. On the remaining two datasets, merging and MTL fail in opposite directions: ISIC, where MTL undershoots the specialist by 4 points and TCGA, where merging undershoots by 9 (finding ii). Neither approach uniformly dominates. Task-vector cosine similarity is informative within a backbone family but does not generalize as a cross-backbone predictor of merge success.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Motivation}

Weight-space merging, which combines multiple fine-tuned models' parameters into a single multi-task model without retraining \cite{ilharco2023editing,yadav2023ties,yu2024language,wortsman2022modelsoupsaveragingweights}, has emerged as an efficient alternative to training joint models. Merging is attractive in clinical deployments because (a) it requires no joint access to the training data, addressing institutional privacy constraints, and (b) it produces a model the same size as one specialist.

Existing merging work, however, is dominated by tasks with relatively low cross-domain gap (different NLP datasets, ImageNet-style image-classification benchmarks). Medical imaging is structurally different. Dermoscopy, chest radiography, and histopathology are nearly disjoint visual domains, and within each dataset there is additional shift from institutions, scanners, and demographics (CheXpert is Stanford, NIH ChestX-ray is NIH, TCGA and ISIC span dozens of contributing sites). Prior merging work \cite{ilharco2023editing,yadav2023ties,yu2024language,du2024parametercompetitionbalancingmodel} predominantly evaluates on tasks that share the pretraining distribution and rarely compares against multi-task joint training as a direct baseline; medical imaging in a cross-domain setting, with MTL as an explicit comparator, is largely unexplored. It is unclear whether prevailing merging methods preserve specialist quality under such heterogeneity, whether the choice of pretrained backbone matters, and whether the choice of merging method should depend on the target task.

We run a controlled study across 7 backbones (CLIP, ViT, DINOv3, RAD-DINO, DINOv2, ViT-MAE, BEiT), 4 medical imaging datasets (ISIC 2017, CheXpert, NIH ChestX-ray, TCGA-LUAD/LUSC), and 8 merging methods \cite{ilharco2023editing,yadav2023ties,yu2024language,matena2022mergingmodelsfisherweightedaveraging,wortsman2022modelsoupsaveragingweights,du2024parametercompetitionbalancingmodel,wang2025linesposttraininglayerscaling}, evaluated against an oracle-router upper bound and a multi-task joint-training (MTL) baseline. We ask where merging preserves specialist quality and where it collapses, and what (if anything) separates the two regimes. 

Our central contribution is to characterize the cross-dataset variance in merging behavior in medical imaging, identify TCGA (lung histopathology) as the one dataset in our grid where merging fails non-trivially, and show that multi-task joint training closes roughly half the resulting gap. As an auxiliary analysis, we test whether task-vector cosine geometry generalizes as a cross-backbone predictor of merge forgetting under leave-one-backbone-out (LOBO) cross-validation, and find that it does not.

\section{Methods}

We fine-tune 7 pretrained ViT backbones (CLIP ViT-B/16, ViT-B/16, DINOv3 ViT-S/16, RAD-DINO, DINOv2-Base, ViT-MAE-Base, BEiT-Base) on 4 medical imaging datasets (ISIC 2017 dermoscopy, CheXpert and NIH ChestX-ray for 5-label radiology, TCGA-LUAD/LUSC for binary lung histopathology). Specialists use AdamW with cosine-warmup and early stopping. Non-CLIP backbones train at LR = 5e-5; CLIP uses 1e-5 (5× lower) to avoid catastrophic forgetting.

We merge task vectors $\tau_i = \theta_{\text{ft},i} - \theta_{\text{pre}}$ across 8 methods (Simple Averaging, Task Arithmetic \cite{ilharco2023editing}, TIES \cite{yadav2023ties}, DARE \cite{yu2024language}, DARE-TIES, PCB-Merging \cite{du2024parametercompetitionbalancingmodel}, LiNeS \cite{wang2025linesposttraininglayerscaling}, and Fisher-Weighted Merging \cite{matena2022mergingmodelsfisherweightedaveraging}), each with a per-method validation-set grid search. Task vectors are computed over the shared ViT encoder parameters. The classification heads remain task-specific (3-class softmax for ISIC, 5-label multilabel for CheXpert and NIH, binary sigmoid for TCGA). At inference, a sample from dataset $d$ is processed by the merged encoder followed by specialist $d$'s head. We compare against two baselines, an oracle router (per-sample routing to the dataset specialist, the upper bound on any merged model) and multi-task joint training (MTL).

We define per-cell merge forgetting $\Delta_d = \text{oracle}_d - \text{merged}_d$ in each dataset's primary metric (balanced accuracy on ISIC, macro-AUROC on CheXpert and NIH, AUROC on TCGA). For the cross-architecture predictor analysis in Appendix~\ref{sec:predictability}, we use leave-one-backbone-out (LOBO) cross-validation, which holds out an entire backbone and evaluates the predictor on it. This matches the regime that arises when planning a merge on a backbone the predictor was not trained on.

\subsection{No merging method dominates}
\label{sec:method-ranking}

Within each dataset, the best-performing merging method varies. Fisher-weighted merging wins ISIC, DARE-TIES wins both radiology datasets (CheXpert and NIH), and PCB-Merging wins lung histopathology (TCGA). Two of these outcomes reflect numerical quirks of Fisher-weighted merging rather than properties of the underlying tasks: ISIC's small validation set produces a Fisher magnitude $120$--$1000\times$ larger than the radiology datasets', so the weighted average is biased toward ISIC-aligned features, and TCGA's binary log-likelihood produces a near-zero Fisher diagonal at the model's confident predictions, so the Fisher merge on TCGA effectively reduces to the Fisher-weighted average of the non-TCGA task vectors. Appendix~C verifies both effects directly and discusses how each should be normalized away in a fairer implementation.

\section{Discussion}

For medical-imaging tasks, considering weight-space merging against its two baselines, routing to a specialist and MTL, the recommendation remains largely dataset-dependent. On the radiology cluster (CheXpert + NIH) all three approaches perform within a percentage point of each other. On the remaining two datasets, merging and MTL fail in opposite directions. TCGA's binary lung-histopathology task vectors are small in norm and idiosyncratic in direction relative to the other tasks (visible in the upper-left of Figure~\ref{fig:norm_vs_cos}). When the four task vectors are averaged, the larger radiology and dermoscopy task vectors dominate and TCGA's small idiosyncratic direction gets diluted out, while joint training keeps the encoder in a region where the TCGA-aligned and radiology/dermoscopy-aligned features can coexist. ISIC pushes the opposite way for different reasons in the two approaches. The best merge ties the specialist only because Fisher-weighted merging dominates the column (the next-best method falls 18 points below) and the validation-set-size artifact in Appendix~C explains the Fisher win. MTL's 3.6-point shortfall is likely gradient interference, where each step does a backward pass per dataset, but ISIC's ~2k samples are cycled ~100× per epoch while CheXpert sees ~200k unique samples per epoch, so radiology gradients dominate the encoder's evolution and ISIC-specific features get overwritten. Neither failure is irreducible, and a properly-tuned MTL with dataset-weighted sampling would likely close the ISIC gap, and merging methods with explicit interference control (PCB, DARE-TIES) already close part of the TCGA gap. Cosine geometry, despite not generalizing as a cross-backbone predictor of merge forgetting (Appendix~B), is useful as a compute-triage signal because the cells with lowest task-vector cosine are also the cells most likely to collapse, so routing them to specialists captures most of the lost accuracy at 50\% compute saved.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

The backbone zoo of $N = 7$ is small. The zoo is also heterogeneous in scale, with DINOv3 at ViT-S/16 ($\sim$22M params) and the other six at ViT-B/16 ($\sim$86M), so cross-backbone comparisons mix scale with pretraining-objective differences. A properly-tuned MTL baseline could change the merge-vs-MTL deltas, especially on ISIC dataset, which is the smallest. Scope is restricted to ViT backbones, $224\times224$ images, classification only, and one-shot rather than continual merging. Several of our datasets are themselves multi-source (TCGA, ISIC), so the cross-domain shift we study is overlaid on uncontrolled within-dataset institutional and scanner shift, which we do not disentangle.

\section{Fisher-weighted merging: numerical artifacts on ISIC and TCGA}

Fisher matrices estimated on small validation sets carry disproportionately large magnitude relative to those estimated on large validation sets, so a Fisher-weighted average across heterogeneous datasets concentrates on whichever dataset has the smallest validation set. Measuring this directly across our grid, ISIC's per-dataset Fisher magnitude (sum of $|F_d|$ over parameters) is between $\mathbf{120\times}$ (RAD-DINO) and $\mathbf{1000\times}$ (DINOv3) larger than the radiology datasets' on every backbone, which is more than enough to dominate the weighted sum even before any other consideration enters. The same Fisher estimator behaves degenerately on TCGA: in our cache the diagonal evaluates to exactly zero, because the gradients of the binary log-likelihood vanish at the model's confident predictions on the dominant class, so the Fisher merge on TCGA collapses to the Fisher-weighted average of the non-TCGA task vectors and effectively contributes nothing from TCGA itself. Both effects can be addressed by normalizing the diagonals by validation-set size before averaging, and the binary-classification case additionally requires a non-degenerate estimator such as a Laplace-style prior on the diagonal to avoid the divide-by-zero collapse.
